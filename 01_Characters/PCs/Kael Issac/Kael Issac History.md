## Hunter's Life
𝐓𝐫𝐢𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐚𝐧 𝐆𝐚𝐥𝐢𝐧𝐚𝐫𝐚𝐞𝐥, 
Hunter's life

After my parents, it was just me and my grandpa. He was a hunter, the kind of man who talks little and noticed everything and his hands steady even in the coldest winds.

He raised me in a log cabin by the Bittermarsh forest edge it was dark, deep and felt endless. He taught me how to stalk without snapping twigs, how to follow the trail of a stag by broken moss or branches, how to gut a rabbit with one clean pull of the knife. Survival was the way he showed me.

Not far off, some...well lot of bandits had their own camp, rough men, loud, always drunk. But they never touched us, used to wonder why. Grandpa never explained. Maybe they feared him. Maybe there was a deal. Either way, they left us alone.

But he had a lot of rules, but no matter what, he told me one rule that I shouldn't break at any cost and that is "never go too deep into the forest." He says it every night when the fire burns low, "Boy, there are things older than men in those woods, never forget that and never go near there."

I thought he was just trying to scare me into staying close. I didn't understand then. Well I do now.
## The Boar Hunt
𝐓𝐫𝐢𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐚𝐧 𝐆𝐚𝐥𝐢𝐧𝐚𝐫𝐚𝐞𝐥, 
The Boar Hunt

I was about to turn eight when grandpa said I was ready to hunt something bigger than a rabbit or pheasant. He handed me a short bow. It was light, small, but the breath felt heavy as it was the mark that I achieved something.

We tracked the boar all morning. The forest was damp with mist, quiet but heavy, like the trees were alive and holding their breath. My chest felt the same way—heavy, nervous, each step louder in my ears than it was on the ground.

We tracked the boar, following clawed-up soil and broken branches, until the stink of it filled the air. And by the seven, that beast was huge. Scars across its side, tusks like swords. Then I almost dropped the bow while shaking. I froze with fear. But my grandpa loosed the first arrow, and it sank deep into the beast. But it turned as its rage fixed on us.

My grandpa shouted, “Steady, boy,” as the beast thundered forward, the earth shaking beneath its hooves. I raised my bow, loosed the arrow as it glanced off its thick hide.

Another breath, the same—and the distance between us vanished, and my body won’t move. I remember the sound of the ground trembling under its charge. I was sure that was it. Eight years old, bow clutched in my hands, a death charging toward me.

But grandpa… he was there before the fear swallowed me whole. He moved quick for an old man, and daggers flashing in his hand, he met the beast head-on. Daggers sank into its eyes. The beast screamed, thrashed, then toppled with a crash that echoed through the trees.

Silence followed. I dropped on the ground as I dropped my bow. My eyes stung though I refused to cry. Grandpa came, lifted me up, his grip strong, steady, grounding me. His words burned themselves into me that day:

“Boy, you shot an arrow which proved ineffective. What should have been done next?”

I stammered, “I should have run and got onto higher ground… a vantage point.”

Grandpa nodded. “As long as you learnt that, boy. Fear is a hunter’s shadow. Don’t let it be the predator that stalks you. Be it a dog that follows you, as you can use it for the hunt. Tame your fear, boy.”

That night we cooked its meat. The taste of smoke and iron stuck in my mouth. I tried to match his strength, but even still, I couldn’t stop shaking. I can hear that charge, the feel of the ground rumbling beneath me. My first brush with death.
## The Ghost in the Woods
𝐓𝐫𝐢𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐚𝐧 𝐆𝐚𝐥𝐢𝐧𝐚𝐫𝐚𝐞𝐥, 
The Ghost in the Woods

I thought I knew fear the day of the boar hunt. I was wrong. Fear had deeper roots than I thought.

It was late autumn. I got used to hunting boars and stag as if the fear was gone. The night grew misty, the trees resembled bones. Grandfather and I were returning from checking traps when I heard it—a whisper, soft, cold, threading between the branches. My skin prickled. I wanted to believe it was just the wind, but the forest felt wrong.

Then I saw it—a pale figure, drifting between the trees. Hollow eyes that burned like coals. I froze.

Grandfather’s hands clamped onto my shoulder as the ghost came toward us. He took out a small bottle from his coat and spread its contents toward the ghost. It shrieked, twisting violently, as my grandfather carried me on his back.

When we reached the cabin, he said, “There are a lot of things that can be hunted, but there are some which we can’t kill without proper tools. And if encountered as such… run always.”

# Path to Shadows
𝐓𝐫𝐢𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐚𝐧 𝐆𝐚𝐥𝐢𝐧𝐚𝐫𝐚𝐞𝐥, 
Path to Shadows

I don’t remember the day I left the cabin, not clearly, what I remember is the silence — the kind that gnaws at you, the kind that makes you realize the fire won’t be lit again, burying me alive. So I left, just as he said.
I walked for days until these dirt path gave way to stone. I thought maybe I’d found something better. I was wrong the city doesn’t feed the hungry as it watches them rot.
I begged, dirty-faced, ribs showing, for bread; people looked past me like I was part of the cobblestone. I slept in the gutters of alleys — and I thought maybe the forest would have been kinder.
That’s when I saw him — a [[rat]], mangy and half-dead trying to steal the crust of bread from bigger rats, standing there, he was dying. I don’t know why I bothered but I threw a stone and scared the bigger rats off. I cried as he bit me for my trouble then ate the crust of bread when he finished, he just stayed. Followed me through the alleys, been with me ever since.
Hunger makes you do things. Begging wasn’t enough. So I observed the crowded markets, careless hands with purses and drunks with coins coming out of taverns. The first time I stole it was an apple. I thought my heart would explode but no one noticed. First became second then third, apple became loaf of bread then pouch of coppers — That’s how I became a thief.
And the other thieves in the city noticed me one night I picked the wrong pocket. Didn’t even see the knife until it was under my chin. They could’ve gutted me, but they didn’t. They said I was with them now.
I tired to run then a hand grabbed my hood and yoked me back saying “nah you’re coming with me” that was [[Robert]].
He was older - not old like my grandfather, but old enough. He smelled of smoke at “you’ll get killed working alone, slow boy” he said “but you’ve got eyes, you pick your pockets well but that won’t be enough  to pick a pocket itself you watch and learn”
And I did
Robert taught me the city like Grandpa taught me the forest — how to move with the crowd, how to breathe in sync with footsteps. So no one hears, how to use shadows, which guards bend for coins which pocket too deep of a pit to avoid, how to tell if the purse was heavy enough to risk.
He laughed when I slipped up, cussed me when I hesitated. To me he was the city’s version of a hunter, where grandpa tracks prey , robert track pockets, Grandpa taught me to read the forest ,robert taught me to read people. And so in a twisted way . It made sense. Survival was survival no matter the ground beneath your feet.
I don’t like him, not like my grandfather but I trusted him and in the alleys, trust is rarer than gold.
